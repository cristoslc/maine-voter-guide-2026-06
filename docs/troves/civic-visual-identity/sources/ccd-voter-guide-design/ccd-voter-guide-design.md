# Designing a voter guide to an election

## Basic design resources

### Fonts for easy-to-read text
You can use any simple sans-serif font, such as Arial, Helvetica, Univers, Verdana, or Calibri.

Some other fonts to consider:
- **Google Noto Sans** — good range of styles and support for most languages. Free via Google Fonts.
- **ClearviewADA** — from Terminal Design. Election offices can use code `clvadaelec` for a 20% discount.

### Colors for election materials
The Center for Civic Design created a palette of colors with good contrast to meet accessibility requirements:
- **7:1 contrast** — for any size text on white, or as a background for white letters
- **3:1 contrast** — for large text (at least 16 points or equivalent)
- **15:1 contrast** — for shaded boxes, suitable for use with black text for good legibility

Downloads:
- [Election design color palette PDF](https://civicdesign.org/wp-content/uploads/2016/02/CCD-Elections-Color-Palette-20160331.pdf)

## Planning the voter guide

Top questions voters ask:
- What's on the ballot?
- What is the date of the next election and voter deadlines?
- What are the options for ways to vote?
- Where do I vote (and hours on Election Day)?
- How do I mark my ballot?
- What are the options for accessible voting?

## Creating the voter guide pages

Page templates (7 page types): covers, Ways to Vote, ballot introduction, candidate statements, measures, practice ballot, blank filler.

Recommended content order:
1. Civics information together toward the front
2. Ballot-related information all together in the second half

Design notes for each page type are linked from the original page.

## Key design takeaways
- Use simple sans-serif fonts
- Ensure minimum 7:1 contrast for body text on white
- Keep civics info front, ballot info back
- Provide templates in multiple formats (Word, InDesign, etc.)
- Check accessibility of each PDF file before distribution
